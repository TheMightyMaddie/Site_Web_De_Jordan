French-Canadian History

The first Europeans to establish themselves as explorers and trading partners with the original inhabitants of what was called New France were, incredibly enough, the French. Their population base focused on what is now Quebec province. This territory was lost to the British in the Seven Years’ War, also known as the French and Indian War, and it presented their new ruler with a dilemma: How do you forcibly convert a large Catholic population that outnumbers your settlers and has friendly relations with various native tribes (now known as First Nations) to accept the Church of England and prevent them from revolting when you have other issues of state that are more pressing?

The Treaty of Paris had in 1763 officially transferred Canada to England, but it was the Quebec Act that guaranteed the French their concessions from the crown, including language, representation, and religion. This same Act of Parliament had a profound effect on the people of the New England area, in that it made peace with the Indians and the French in part by declaring the Ohio Valley off limits to their western expansionism, which along with other Intolerable Acts set off the American Revolution.

Even as Canada now became populated with more or less exclusively Protestant immigrants from England, Wales, Scotland, and later other parts of Europe, the French Canadians kept their identity and culture distinct. French remained an official language in the province and was later made an official language of Canada along with English. Official government communiques are in both languages.

French is Taught in Schools throughout Canada

Students learn French as a subject. At the elementary level, students must accumulate a minimum of 600 hours of French instruction by the end of Grade 8. At the secondary level, academic, applied and open courses are offered for Grades 9 and 10.

Student’s proficiency in French increases based on the amount of time and level of intensity of instruction in French. The French Second Language (FSL) programs help students develop an appreciation of the language and French culture and the world from a young age and continues to foster it through the majority of their academic careers.

French and English are the Official Languages of Canada

French is the mother tongue of about 7.2 million Canadians, according to Census Canada 2016. By the Official Languages Act in 1969, Canada recognized English and French as having equal status in the government of Canada. The Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms requires all provinces and territories to provide primary and secondary education to their official-language minorities.

Learning French is encouraged for many beneficial reasons, such as strengthening critical thinking skills, developing understanding of diverse cultures, and increasing competitiveness in the global market.

For these reasons and many more, it is required for schools to teach both English and French.
